# 2. The economics of automation in 2026

A short version of why this is the right time:

Three forces moved together in the last twenty-four months:

1. **Frontier-grade models became commoditised.** What cost $0.20 per call in 2023 costs less than $0.001 today for equivalent quality. A voice agent that handles a five-minute call costs cents. A classifier that reads an email and decides whether to escalate costs a fraction of that. Inference cost is no longer a barrier to deploying automation at the per-task level.
2. **Orchestration tooling caught up.** n8n, Temporal, Inngest, and a long tail of workflow engines made it routine to chain together API calls, model invocations, retries, human-in-the-loop checkpoints, and observability. The infrastructure problem that used to consume sixty percent of an automation project's budget now consumes ten.
3. **Operator literacy improved.** Your team has used ChatGPT enough that "talk to a model" is not a strange instruction anymore. Adoption — the thing that historically killed automation projects after they shipped — is the easiest it has ever been.

What this means in practice: the question is no longer "can we automate this?" It is "which one of the dozen things we could automate this quarter is the one that has the largest unit-economic payoff if we automate it well?"

That is the conversation we have with clients every week. The rest of this document is what we have learned to offer when that conversation goes well.

### The unit of automation we measure

We use a single number to compare every project: **hours back per week, multiplied by fifty weeks, divided by fully-loaded cost of the person whose hours we recovered.** That gives a per-engagement annual savings number that pays for the build many times over within the first quarter. We will not start a project we cannot model this way.

### The frontier we will not promise

We will not build automation that pretends to do work it cannot do. If a workflow needs a human at the end to verify, we keep the human in. If a model is wrong twenty percent of the time, we route low-confidence outputs to a human queue, not to a customer. Bots that confidently fabricate are the single fastest way to destroy a brand we have seen, and we have a written policy against shipping them.

# 4. Browser & web automation

The internet remains, for many operations, the cheapest data source on earth. Most of it is sitting behind forms, paginations, login walls, and javascript that breaks every Tuesday. We build the layer that gets your team — or your agents — past all of that, reliably.

### OpenClaw — the open-source browser agent we contribute to

OpenClaw is a real-browser automation framework we deploy heavily. It runs an actual Chromium instance, supports human-in-the-loop intervention when a workflow encounters a captcha or a 2FA prompt, and persists session state across runs.

We use OpenClaw for:

- **Account-gated data extraction.** Some of the most valuable competitive intelligence on the internet is behind a login. OpenClaw runs a real browser, signs in once, and harvests on a schedule.
- **Procurement and pricing monitoring.** Daily checks of competitor pricing on SaaS sites, marketplaces, RFP portals. Differences trigger alerts before your sales team finds out from a customer.
- **Government-portal interactions.** Filing forms, retrieving filings, monitoring docket changes. The last decade of web modernisation forgot the public sector; OpenClaw bridges that gap.
- **Slow, complex workflows that no API exists for.** Anything a human does in a browser, OpenClaw can do — slowly, predictably, and with screenshots at every step so the audit trail is unambiguous.

### Hermes — the LLM-driven exploration agent

Hermes is the second pillar of our browser work. Where OpenClaw runs scripted flows, Hermes drives a browser using an LLM that decides what to click, what to read, and when to give up. We deploy Hermes when:

- The site changes weekly and writing brittle selectors is more expensive than tolerating a slower-but-flexible agent.
- The task involves "find me everything about X" rather than "run these eight steps."
- The user wants a natural-language interface to a structured action ("download the last three invoices, summarise the differences").

### Custom Playwright / Puppeteer pipelines

When the workflow is well-understood and the site is stable, we still write classic Playwright scripts. They are the fastest, most reliable, and most maintainable option in that regime. We use OpenClaw and Hermes where they are better, not for the sake of using AI.

### Anti-bot, captcha, and proxy strategy

We deal with anti-bot infrastructure honestly. Where the site's terms permit automation, we use residential proxies, rotation, and human-like timing. Where they do not, we will tell you the cost of compliance and let you decide. We will not ship workflows that knowingly violate a target site's TOS without your explicit informed consent in writing.

### Observability and resilience

Every browser workflow we ship comes with:

- A run log persisted to your storage, screenshots included.
- An anomaly alert if the run duration deviates from the baseline by more than thirty percent.
- A retry policy with exponential backoff and a human-escalation queue when retries are exhausted.
- A monthly health report describing how often the workflow ran, succeeded, retried, and was manually intervened on.

### What we charge

A scripted Playwright pipeline against a stable site: typically $8,000–$15,000 to build, $200–$800/month to run depending on volume and proxy needs.

An OpenClaw or Hermes deployment with login state, complex flows, and human-in-the-loop fallback: $18,000–$30,000 to build, $800–$2,500/month to run.

The pricing is not magic. We base it on engineering hours plus the per-run infrastructure cost of the proxies, model inference, and storage.

# 5. Voice agents

The single biggest shift in 2026 was the maturation of voice models. Latency dropped under 600ms. Interruption handling became routine. In-language support went from a six-figure project to a three-week build. The economics of an inbound or outbound phone operation reshaped overnight.

### What we build

**Inbound triage and tier-1 deflection.** Your support line picks up. The agent understands the caller's intent, looks up their account from your CRM, attempts the most likely resolution, and transfers to a human only when the script branches into a regulatory, complex, or high-value lane. Typical deflection: 60–80% of calls handled without a human touching them.

**Outbound qualification and follow-up.** The agent calls a list — leads, dormant customers, lapsed renewals — and conducts a structured conversation that ends with a calendar booking, an updated CRM record, or a polite goodbye. We have shipped outbound flows that touched 4,000 contacts a day at a per-call cost of well under a dollar fully loaded.

**Booking and rescheduling.** The agent owns your calendar. Calls in want appointments, the agent checks availability, books, and texts confirmation. Calls in want to reschedule, the agent reschedules. The handful of edge cases — emergency cancellations, custom-pricing questions — go to a human.

**Multi-language operations.** We deploy English, Spanish, Hindi, Arabic, French, and a long tail of others. The model picks up the caller's language in the first three seconds and the rest of the call follows in that language without anyone having to explicitly select it.

### How we make voice agents not sound like robots

We have shipped enough voice agents to know what kills them. Three things:

1. **Latency.** Anything over 800ms feels uncanny. We target 400–600ms end-to-end and we test under real network conditions, not just on a fibre line in our office.
2. **Backchannels.** Real people say "mhm" and "got it" and "give me a second". Our agents do too. We tune the backchannel frequency to match the use case (more in support, less in cold outbound).
3. **Graceful failure.** When an agent does not understand, it does not say "I'm sorry, can you repeat that?" thirteen times. After two confused exchanges it transfers to a human, hands over the context, and the caller never gets stuck.

### Integration with your existing telephony

We work with Twilio, Vonage, Telnyx, and most of the major SIP vendors. If you already have a Five9, Genesys, or Aircall setup, we can usually slot the agent in as a worker on your existing queue rather than rebuilding the routing.

### Pricing

Build: $12,000–$30,000 for an inbound triage agent on one queue. $25,000–$60,000 for a multi-queue, multi-language deployment with full CRM integration. Outbound campaigns: $15,000–$40,000 for the build, plus per-minute infrastructure costs (typically $0.05–$0.12 per minute fully loaded depending on language and model choice).

### A real number from a real engagement

A home-services client running ~3,000 inbound calls a month: previously two full-time agents at $52K each, plus overflow agency at $14K. Total annual: $118K. Post-deployment: one part-time agent at $26K, plus the voice agent infrastructure at $1,600/month ($19.2K). Total annual: $45.2K. Annual savings: $72.8K. Build cost: $22K. Payback: just under four months.

We do not show this number to over-promise. We show it because the math is what closes the conversation.

# 6. Email automation

Email is where most service businesses lose the most hours. People type the same response twenty-five times a day, get to inbox-zero on Friday at six, and start over Monday at eight. We automate that.

### Our flagship product: hiagents

hiagents is our own multi-tenant SaaS that we operate for clients. It is an AI auto-responder for client and customer queries that:

- Connects to your Gmail (or Workspace) via OAuth.
- Polls your inbox every minute for new mail.
- Classifies each message as a real customer query or noise (newsletters, internal threads, automated notifications).
- Retrieves the relevant passages from a private knowledge base (PDFs of your service catalogue, policies, FAQ, whitever you upload).
- Generates a reply in your tone and voice, grounded only in what the knowledge base says.
- Sends — or drafts, your choice — the reply in-thread.
- Logs every step for audit and review.

We built hiagents because we needed it ourselves. Our agency mailbox was getting forty-plus client questions a week and the team was answering each one from scratch. We are now signing up other agencies, law firms, real-estate brokers, and SaaS support teams to the same tool.

### What hiagents does not do

It will not invent answers. If your knowledge base does not cover a question, the bot will not reply — it tags the email and waits for a human. We made that decision early because we have seen too many auto-responders confidently make things up and lose customers permanently.

It will not reply on your behalf without a knowledge base. The whole product is grounded retrieval. If you have nothing for it to retrieve from, it does nothing.

It will not handle complex multi-turn negotiations. Auto-responders are for questions with answers. They are not for sales conversations that need a human to read the room.

### Custom email automation for higher-stakes flows

hiagents is the off-the-shelf version. For clients with more complex needs we build custom email pipelines. Examples:

- **Refund triage for an e-commerce business.** Inbound refund requests are parsed, the order is looked up in Shopify, the reason is classified, and the system either auto-issues the refund (small dollar, policy-compliant), escalates to a human (larger dollar or edge-case), or replies with a clarifying question. Built for one client in three weeks, handles ~600 refund requests a week, escalates 12% to humans.
- **Vendor onboarding email coordination.** A new vendor sends contract paperwork. The system extracts the relevant fields, populates the internal record, sends back the next form, and chases on a cadence if the vendor goes silent. Reduced average vendor-onboarding time from 11 days to 3.
- **Calendar email parsing.** Inbound emails with vague scheduling intent ("can we chat next week?") are converted into proposed slots, sent back as a click-to-book link, and the calendar event is created on confirmation. The most boring thing we have ever automated. Saves the average operator two hours a week.

### Pricing

hiagents (managed SaaS): pilot is $1,500 for the first month, then $400/month flat for SMB use, plus LLM usage at cost (typically $20–$80/month for a normal inbox).

Custom email pipelines: $6,000–$25,000 build depending on complexity, $200–$800/month to run.

# 7. Document & knowledge AI

The second-biggest unrecognised cost in most businesses is the time someone spends looking for a document or a clause or a number that is technically findable but practically lost. We build the system that finds it in two seconds.

### Use cases we have shipped

**Contract intelligence.** A law firm uploads its standard MSAs, NDAs, and SOWs. The system extracts every variable clause, indexes the wording, and lets the firm answer questions like "in how many of our active NDAs did we agree to a five-year confidentiality term?" without anyone manually reading PDFs.

**Internal Q&A bot grounded in policy and runbooks.** Operations teams used to onboard new hires by handing them a 200-page wiki and praying. We replace that with a Q&A bot that the new hire can ask anything, grounded only in your internal policies. Wrong answer? You update the policy doc, not the bot.

**Sales collateral retrieval.** Account executives ask "what's our security story for HIPAA-regulated buyers?" and the system returns the exact paragraph from the security whitepaper, plus a one-line summary. Saves the AE five minutes per question and avoids the worst alternative — the AE making it up on the call.

**Compliance research.** Regulatory bodies publish a lot of text. We index it, set up alerts on changes that affect a client's filings, and produce a weekly summary in plain English. We have done this for financial-services clients and for one health-tech client.

### How we build it

We do not sell a product called "RAG-as-a-service." We build a fitting-the-business solution every time, because the wrong chunk size, the wrong embedding model, the wrong reranker, and the wrong evaluation methodology kill these systems faster than any other automation type. Our typical build includes:

- Document ingestion pipeline with format-specific extraction (PDF, DOCX, HTML, scanned images via OCR).
- Embedding generation with one of OpenAI, Cohere, Voyage, or a local model depending on your privacy posture.
- Vector store in Supabase pgvector, Pinecone, Weaviate, or Qdrant.
- Retrieval with hybrid keyword-plus-vector and a reranker on top.
- An eval harness so we (and you) can measure retrieval quality over time as the corpus grows.
- A small UI for the user-facing query and a wider one for the operator who maintains the corpus.

### Pricing

Pilot on one corpus: $10,000–$15,000. Production deployment with eval harness, corpus management, and access controls: $18,000–$40,000. Monthly run cost: typically $150–$1,000 depending on query volume and corpus size.

# 8. Workflow & integration glue

This is the unglamorous middle of our practice. The data has to move. The events have to fire. The two SaaS tools that refuse to talk to each other have to talk through us. We do a lot of this work.

### n8n workflow builds

n8n is our default workflow engine. It is open-source, self-hostable, has the breadth of Zapier with the flexibility of code, and we can stand it up on a $20-a-month VPS for any client who wants to own their automation infrastructure outright.

Typical n8n projects:

- **Lead intake from any source into your CRM.** Web form, Calendly, Linkedin DM extract, conference attendee list — anything that produces a lead gets normalised, deduplicated, enriched, and inserted into the CRM with the right owner, tags, and follow-up sequence.
- **Cross-tool sync.** Stripe customer becomes HubSpot contact becomes a Notion record becomes a Slack notification becomes a calendar invite for kickoff. The chain that nobody on your team enjoys building or maintaining.
- **Internal report glue.** Pull data from your data warehouse, run aggregations, format as a Slack-ready summary, post on a schedule.
- **Failure-recovery pipelines.** When tool X is down, route to tool Y. When the primary data source is stale, fall back to the cached version. The boring resilience work that prevents 3am pages.

### Zapier replacements (for cost or capability reasons)

We migrate clients off Zapier when they hit one of three walls: the per-task pricing has crossed $500/month, the flows need conditional logic Zapier cannot express, or they need to self-host for compliance. The migration is usually under three weeks for a portfolio of ~25 zaps and pays for itself in under a year.

### Custom API integration work

When n8n is not the right fit (high volume, custom rate-limiting, complex state machines), we write code. Node, Python, or Go depending on what your team already supports. We do not insist on a particular stack for its own sake — we use what your engineering team can maintain.

### Webhooks, queues, and event-driven plumbing

For clients with engineering teams, we design the event-driven layer that sits between their products and their tools. Inngest, Temporal, BullMQ, or whatever fits. The deliverable is a documented event topology that someone other than us can extend in six months.

### Pricing

Single n8n workflow: $1,500–$5,000 depending on complexity. Portfolio of 10–20 workflows: $8,000–$18,000 (we apply a portfolio discount). Custom API work: hourly, typically $150–$250 depending on engineer seniority, with a fixed-bid option after the spec is locked.

# 9. Customer support automation

Support is the operation where automation pays back fastest and where bad automation hurts the most. Every project here is built with the principle that humans get a clean handoff when the bot cannot help, with full context, not a dropped ticket.

### Tier-1 deflection

A clear, useful answer in seconds for the questions you answer twenty times a day. Order status, password resets, return policy questions, hours of operation, simple billing questions. We deflect with a Q&A system grounded in your help centre, with explicit fallback to a human when confidence drops below a threshold you set.

### Triage and routing

Inbound tickets are read, classified by topic and urgency, enriched with the customer's account context, and routed to the right queue with a suggested response drafted. Your agent's first action is to confirm or edit the draft, not to write from scratch. Typical time-per-ticket reduction: 40–60%.

### Escalation intelligence

When the bot escalates, it writes a one-paragraph summary of what the customer asked, what was tried, and what the system suspects is going on. Your senior support person starts the ticket already half-resolved. We have shipped this for clients whose senior support was about to quit from burnout; the burnout symptom that drives those quits is the "starting from scratch" feeling, and a good summary kills it.

### Macro generation

For the patterns the team keeps answering, we generate macros from your past tickets. The macros are reviewed by a human (we will not auto-publish them) and joined to your support tool as one-click responses.

### Sentiment and risk detection

Inbound messages flagged for anger, fraud indicators, or threats of churn get routed to a dedicated queue with response-time SLAs. Most clients do not realise how many of those signals were buried in their general queue until they see the new dashboard.

### Multi-channel coordination

Support is no longer one channel. We tie email, chat, voice, social DMs, and in-app messages into one inbox-like view, with the AI doing the cross-channel deduplication ("this is the same person asking the same question on three platforms").

### Pricing

Tier-1 deflection on one channel: $10,000–$18,000. Multi-channel triage + escalation + macros: $20,000–$35,000. Ongoing tuning retainer (recommended for the first six months): $1,500–$3,000/month.

We deploy these for clients running outbound on volumes from 500 to 50,000 contacts a month. An agent stack at $1,800/month produces 30,000+ at comparable quality. We are aware those numbers are easy to misrepresent; we model your specific economics in the pilot.

Take a name, a company, or a domain. Return verified contact info, firmographic signal, intent signal (job postings, hiring spikes, funding events, news), and an ICP fit score. We integrate with Apollo, Clearbit, ZoomInfo, LinkedIn (where ToS-compliant), and a long tail of niche sources.

### CRM hygiene

The dirty open secret of every sales operation: your CRM is half-correct on the best day. We build the agent that audits records, flags inconsistencies (this contact has three different emails, this account has a duplicate, this stage has been "in negotiation" for 187 days), and either auto-corrects or queues for human review.

### Pipeline intelligence

Beyond hygiene: agents that read every email and call transcript tied to a deal, write a fresh deal-state summary, flag stalled deals, and surface the specific risk for each one ("buyer last replied 16 days ago, last message asked for security questionnaire, none has been sent"). Sales managers read one dashboard a day instead of fifty deal pages.

### Forecasting assistance

Not the model itself — we trust your existing forecasting model — but the data-quality layer underneath it. Agents that make sure every deal has the fields the forecast needs, with values that are consistent with the actual evidence in the deal's history.

### Pricing

# 17. Our process

We use the same four-step process on every engagement. It is short, deliberate, and we do not skip steps.

### Step one: read like a buyer

Before any tool gets opened, we sit down with you (and one or two operators who actually do the work) and write down:

- The five tasks that consume the most hours on the team this quarter.
- The cost per occurrence of each (in minutes, in errors, in customer-felt impact).
- The current workaround, if any.
- The dependencies that would have to hold for an automation to actually fix this.

This document is the only thing we agree to be measured against.

### Step two: map the workflow

We diagram the current state and the future state. Where the decisions happen. Where the data crosses systems. Where the model goes and where it does not. The map is a one-page artifact your team can read in three minutes.

We do not ship without the map. If the map is not clear, the build is not ready.

### Step three: build

We build in small commits. Daily updates on what shipped, what is blocked, what we learned. Most of our engagements run two to six weeks of build time depending on scope. We do not disappear for a month and surface with a demo.

Throughout the build, we run a staging deployment against your real data (with read-only or sandboxed write access) so you can see the system behave on actual conditions.

### Step four: hand over

We write:

- An operator guide: what the system does, how to use it, what to expect.
- A runbook: what to do when X breaks, who to call, what to read first.
- A credential inventory: every API key, every account, every where-it-lives note.
- A handover call: live walkthrough with the operator and an engineering stakeholder.

After handover, you can keep us on retainer for ongoing changes (most clients do for the first three to six months) or run the system independently. We do not make either path a captive choice.

# 22. Case studies

### Case study one: voice-agent triage for a home services franchise

The franchise was running 12 regional offices, each fielding 200–400 calls a week, with 16 full-time call agents and three part-time supervisors. Peak hours overflowed to a paid answering service at $4.20 per call. Average customer wait time on a Monday was 11 minutes; lost-call rate was 14% on average.

We deployed an inbound voice agent on a single shared SIP trunk that:

- Answered every call in under three rings.
- Identified the caller's intent within the first 20 seconds.
- Booked simple service-call appointments directly (a quarter of inbound).
- Transferred urgent matters (a tenth of inbound) to a human agent with full context.
- Routed status checks and rescheduling to a self-serve flow.
- Routed everything else into the existing call-center queue.

Twelve weeks post-deployment:

- Average wait time on a Monday: 1 minute 40 seconds.
- Lost-call rate: 3%.
- Call-agent headcount reduced from 16 FTE to 11 FTE through natural attrition.
- Overflow answering service replaced. Spend went from $32,000/month to $4,800/month.
- Net annual savings: $608,000 against a build-and-first-year-run cost of $42,000.

The voice agent's accuracy on intent classification, measured against a human reviewer sample of 200 calls a month, was 96%.

### Case study two: knowledge-base assistant for a law firm

A boutique firm had a thousand-plus PDF library of templates, redlines, prior agreements, and client memos. Junior associates spent an hour per assignment searching for relevant clauses. The senior partners noticed because clients noticed.

We built a knowledge-base assistant that:

- Ingested the full library with format-aware extraction.
- Used a domain-specific embedding tuned on the firm's prior work.
- Provided a chat interface where any associate could ask "have we agreed to a non-solicit longer than 12 months in any contract since 2022?" and get back a list of the seven instances with one-line context for each.
- Logged every query for partner review.

Quantified outcomes:

- Average time-per-research-task: 56 minutes → 4 minutes.
- Hours recovered across the firm per week: ~80.
- Junior associates rated the tool 9.1/10 on usefulness in a six-month survey.
- One regulatory audit that previously consumed three associates for a week was completed by one associate in two days.

Build cost: $34,000. Monthly run cost: $620.

### Case study three: cross-system data sync for a multi-brand e-commerce operator

The client ran four DTC brands on three Shopify stores, with customer data in Klaviyo, support tickets in Gorgias, fulfilment in ShipBob, and inventory in a custom Notion+Airtable hybrid that no one fully owned.

The problem they came to us with: marketing decisions for one brand were being made on stale data because no one trusted what was in any one tool. The chief of staff was spending eight hours a week reconciling.

We built:

- A unified customer record system (a small Postgres database, hosted on Supabase) ingesting from all four sources nightly.
- A conflict-resolution policy: most-recent-wins for contact info, longest-history-wins for lifetime value attribution, source-of-truth for inventory was ShipBob (overriding the Airtable/Notion hybrid).

- Marketing campaigns ran on data updated nightly instead of weekly.
- Reconciliation time: 8 hours/week → 30 minutes/week.
- Inventory-driven misorders (where a brand listed an out-of-stock SKU as available): 14/month → 1/month.

Build cost: $28,000. Monthly run cost: $480.
